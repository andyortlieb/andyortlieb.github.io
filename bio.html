The Technological Biography for Andy Ortlieb

Growing Up

I started tinkering with computers and their peripherals once I was given my first computer in 1989. Since then I was always intrigued by how they worked, why they were useful, and how they could be modified.

The Apple IIc+... 1989-1996

As a young child I was given my first computer in 1989, an Apple IIc+ with a Color Apple Monitor II and a Color ImageWriter II! Growing up in a household that didn't have much money, coupled with the fact that that particular Apple model was a bit of a flop, there wasn't much for me to do with it besides figure out how it worked.

So it became a mild interest and hobby of mine to explore every reach of every mundane piece of software that we could stuff into the diskette box. It didn't take long for me to start making sense of the user manuals and other documentation that the machine came with. The Packard Bell Windows 95 PC 1996-2000

In 1996 we would finally modernize our computer desk with a Packard Bell. This particular machine came with some bizarre alternative GUI shell called "navigator", which immediately taught me that we do not have to be satisfied with the software & configurations that manufacturers set is up with, because that thing was goofy as all get out. From here it wouldn't be long before I rediscovered my infatuation for systems like I did several years earlier with the Apple II.

Networking 1996-

Having a computer that came with a 28.8 Kbps modem, and a never ending stream of free trial floppy disks from a variety of competing nanny-state Internet Service Providers, I had an opportunity to learn how to create websites, meet new people from far away with similar interests, discovering the RFC's, and loads of texts published ad-hoc.

Occasionally the trial periods would run dry, or connectivity would just not be in the family budget. These were the times when I would join forces with my cousin to figure out how to make our computers talk to eachother over the phone without requiring money. This would prove to lead to more interest in connectivity, how it works, and what can be done with it.

Programming 1996-

Besides tinkering with Apple BASIC, I had an opportunity to learn Pascal (Borland TurboPascal) with the help of my Uncle, a chemical engineer who used it for his calculations and simulations. As a tween I couldn't find too much use for it except to make simple trivia games for bands that I liked at the time.

After becoming connected online, however, I discovered the potential usefulness for code in the form of IRC scripts, manipulation of client software for certain internet service providers, as well as simple tools to keep my computer organized.

Multimedia 1996-

It didn't take much exploring to discover the sndrec32.exe and to have the realization that there must also be better software for recording. This prompted me to learn how to create multi-track musical recordings of myself playing various instruments, as well as composition with MIDI.

While I wasn't privy to any contemporary design disciplines, my teenage self would be sure to create the edgiest of geocities websites backdropped by wavy magenta tesselations with animated borders hand-drawn frame-by-frame and pixel-by-pixel (and no, I am not bragging).

Linux 1997-

At some point I became convinced by people and text files that a UNIX operating system was necessary in order to interact with and fully understand things that I was becoming interested in.

I started playing with a free shell account on SDF (Lonestar.org) to learn my way around the shell. But it never felt like it belonged to me (and I had a hard time understanding why they were letting me use their computer), so I decided I had to figure out how to set up my own box.

I, a person with an x86 machine of course had to figure out how to run Linux all on my own. I discovered the book "Slackware 96 Unleashed", which helped me get installed on a shared partition with Windows using the "Umsdos" filesystem. While it was a struggle to get it online with my skillset at the time, I did eventually manage to play a graphical Asteroids clone, and run the GIMP.

Between 2000 through 2006 I would toggle between Windows and Linux as my daily driver for weeks or months at a time, which would take me to an extreme love-hate relationship with open source software. After 2006 I would not run Windows on my machines at home again until around 2017.

Career

See Also: Commercial Products

I began my career in technology around 2005, when I started a data-imaging position for a local medical billing firm. From there, I was almost immediately promoted to a systems administrator position after suggesting and implementing a number of process changes that would streamline throughput and increase accuracy.

In this position I learned how to manage Active Directory, Disaster Recovery, Linux and SCO UNIX systems, and websites with SSL. I also administered plenty of industry specific software packages. The need to develop new systems to interact with clients and partners showed up pretty quickly as I developed customer portals and automation systems, and learned how to use relational databases.
